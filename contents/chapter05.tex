\subsection{Observations}

Based on the results presented in Table \ref{tab:thumb_model_comparison} and the ROC curves in Figure \ref{fig:thumb_roc_comparison}(a) and (b), in the 2020 dataset, tree-based models such as Random Forest and XGBoost demonstrated superior discriminative power, both achieving an AUROC of 0.77. However, evaluated on the 2025 dataset, Logistic Regression proved more resilient, securing the highest accuracy (0.77) and F1-score (0.56). This shift indicates that simpler linear models may provide better cross-dataset stability, whereas complex tree models suffer from significant performance degradation.

Furthermore, Table \ref{tab:thumb_cross_dataset} and Figure \ref{fig:thumb_roc_comparison}(c) illustrate the results of explicit cross-cohort evaluation (trained on 2020, tested on 2025). While Random Forest achieved the highest accuracy (0.80), its discriminative balance remained limited (F1-score of 0.52). XGBoost provided the highest AUROC (0.71), suggesting it yields better instance ranking despite lower absolute recall. In contrast to internal validation, Logistic Regression struggled in this direct transfer scenario, indicating that linear assumptions may be insufficient for generalizing complex fingertip patterns across distinct temporal cohorts.

\subsection{Observations}

Based on the results of utilizing bilateral hand joint features across three machine learning models shown in Table \ref{tab:bilateral_model_comparison}, Random Forest and Logistic Regression achieved strong results on the 2020 dataset, with AUROCs of 0.81 and 0.80, respectively. Logistic Regression led in accuracy (0.79) and F1-score (0.66), suggesting that a balance of features from both hands is well-suited to linear classification. When evaluating the 2025 dataset, Random Forest emerged as the most resilient model, maintaining a stable accuracy of 0.71 and yielding the highest AUROC (0.71), whereas Logistic Regression, despite holding a high accuracy (0.76), saw sharp declines in predictive balance. Overall, tree-based models like Random Forest demonstrated superior robustness in generalizing bilateral features across distinct cohorts.

The cross-dataset performance in Table \ref{tab:bilateral_cross_dataset} shows a similar trend where tree-based models (XGBoost and Random Forest) outperform Logistic Regression in terms of AUROC (0.69 and 0.68 respectively) when trained on 2020 and tested on 2025. Although Logistic Regression maintained a competitive accuracy of 0.70, its AUROC dropped to 0.50, indicating a loss in its ability to differentiate between classes in a new temporal cohort compared to tree-based models.

SelectKBest and Logistic L1 also maintain respectable AUROCs of 0.68, though Logistic L1 achieves a slightly better F1-score (0.50). These results underscore that tree-based feature selection can be quite effective for ensuring accuracy in cross-dataset robustness, even if discriminative ranking power (AUROC) varies.

The AUROC values lie between 0.53 and 0.62, with XGBoost integrated into the Same Block design achieving the highest AUROC (0.62) and Accuracy (0.64). The sharp decline in F1-scores ($\sim$0.40) and AUROC compared to the internal splits suggests that while GCNs are extremely powerful within a dataset, the structural features extracted may be sensitive to shifts in the data distribution between the 2020 and 2025 collection periods, requiring potentially more robust feature alignment or domain adaptation strategies for direct cross-dataset transfer.
